Your Pregnancy Wellness Guide

COMPILED MARCH 2026  ·  SOURCES: ACOG, NIH, COCHRANE, PUBMED

For Kathryn — Your body is doing extraordinary things. This guide is here to help you decode what works for YOUR ecosystem during this journey. Every body is different. The science here is strong, but you are the expert on your own signals. Use this as a map, not a manual.

— J
Quick Reference

Top 10 Takeaways

The ten behaviors with the broadest evidence base — each affecting multiple symptoms simultaneously and carrying the lowest risk. If you do nothing else, do these.

CH-01 · NUTRITION
01

Take a high-quality prenatal vitamin — now

Folic acid prevents neural tube defects. Iron prevents fatigue. Iodine supports brain development. Most prenatals underdose vitamin D, choline, and omega-3 — supplement those separately. Verify 150 mcg iodine is present.

CH-02 · MOVEMENT
02

150 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise per week

The single intervention with the most overlapping benefits: reduces GDM risk, preeclampsia risk, back pain, depression, anxiety, constipation, fatigue, and sleep disruption. Walking is sufficient. Start in the first trimester, maintain through delivery.

CH-03 · RESILIENCE
03

Pelvic floor training from day one

3 sets of 10–15 Kegel contractions, 3x/day. Evidence shows reduced urinary incontinence and possible reduction in episiotomy and perineal tearing rates. Most women skip this. Don't.

04

Eat small, frequent, nutrient-dense meals (5–6/day)

Manages nausea, prevents heartburn, stabilizes blood sugar, and supports consistent fetal nutrient delivery. This single structural change addresses three of the most common adverse symptoms.

05

Sleep left-side with a pregnancy pillow from T2

IPD meta-analysis links supine sleep with increased stillbirth risk. Left-side positioning also improves IVC blood flow, reduces edema, and reduces heartburn. A pregnancy pillow dramatically improves compliance.

06

Optimize iron intake and absorption

Iron deficiency is the most common reversible cause of pregnancy fatigue. 27 mg/day. Pair with vitamin C; avoid calcium and coffee/tea within 1 hour of iron intake. Get ferritin checked — below 30 ng/mL warrants supplementation.

07

Supplement DHA/omega-3 separately (600–1,000 mg/day)

Fetal brain and retina development depend on DHA, especially in the third trimester. Most prenatal vitamins contain inadequate amounts. A 2026 study confirmed omega-3 independently reduces preterm birth rates. Choose algae-based DHA if not eating fatty fish 2–3x/week.

08

Drink 8–12 cups of water daily — consistently

Supports amniotic fluid volume, prevents constipation, reduces UTI risk, reduces edema, and may reduce preeclampsia risk. Paradoxically, drinking more water reduces water retention. Pale yellow urine is your practical target.

09

Actively manage stress with one evidence-based practice

Chronic stress is a physiological risk factor, not just discomfort. Elevated cortisol is associated with preterm birth and low birth weight. Mindfulness programs, exercise, social support, and CBT-I all have evidence. Build at least one into daily routine.

10

Know the warning signs and seek care early

No lifestyle protocol replaces prenatal care. Preeclampsia, preterm labor, GDM complications, and perinatal depression are all significantly better when caught early. Attend all appointments. Track fetal movement in T3. Advocate for yourself.

Your body is running the most complex experiment it will ever run. Your job is to create the conditions for it to succeed.

Weeks 1–13

First Trimester

Build the Foundation

Nutrition

  • Start prenatal vitamin immediately
  • Verify folic acid and iodine coverage
  • Don't stress calories if nausea is severe — focus on tolerating food
  • Small, frequent meals; cold foods often more tolerable (less smell)
  • Ginger and B6 first-line for nausea
  • Begin iron-conscious eating: lean meats, legumes, leafy greens + vitamin C

Exercise

  • Continue pre-pregnancy exercise
  • If new: start with 10–15 min walks and build slowly
  • Honor severe fatigue in weeks 6–10
  • Begin pelvic floor exercises — start early, maintain throughout
  • Stay cool; exercise in climate-controlled environments

Sleep & Stress

  • Lean into natural early bedtimes that fatigue enforces
  • Begin left-side sleeping habit before it becomes structural
  • Anxiety about the pregnancy is common and treatable — address it early

Key Appointments

  • First prenatal: typically weeks 8–10
  • Discuss supplements, genetic screening, exercise contraindications
Weeks 14–27

Second Trimester

The Performance Window

Nutrition

  • Increase to +340 calories/day — nutrient-dense sources
  • DHA demand increases as fetal brain development accelerates (600–1,000 mg/day)
  • Choline critical for this window — eggs daily if tolerated
  • Monitor iron status with provider (blood volume expands rapidly)
  • Begin heartburn management: smaller meals, avoid triggers, elevate head of bed
  • Target 25–30g fiber/day to stay ahead of constipation

Exercise

  • Energy typically returns after week 14 — best trimester for fitness
  • Transition exercises off supine positions
  • Modify core: replace crunches with bird-dogs, standing core
  • Introduce prenatal yoga for back pain + mental health
  • Functional strength: squats, hinges, rows

Sleep

  • Begin using pregnancy pillow as belly growth disrupts natural position
  • If RLS emerges, discuss ferritin levels with provider

New Symptoms to Watch

  • Back pain emerges mid-T2 — begin stability work before it's severe
  • Round ligament pain peaks weeks 14–20 — move slowly
  • Heartburn often begins week 20+ — implement dietary strategies preemptively
Weeks 28–40

Third Trimester

Prepare and Protect

Nutrition

  • Increase to +450 calories/day; protein targets critical
  • If GDM screening positive: LGI diet + consistent exercise immediately
  • Hydration critical — amniotic fluid volume depends on it
  • Small, frequent meals essential — stomach physically compressed
  • Continue DHA — highest brain accumulation occurs in T3

Exercise

  • Walking and swimming most universally appropriate
  • Reduce impact; avoid high-fall-risk activities
  • Birth prep movements: hip opening, cat-cow, supported squats
  • Continue pelvic floor work; add perineal massage after week 34
  • Vigorous exercise remains safe if you feel well and have no contraindications

Sleep

  • Full pregnancy pillow for complete support
  • Left-side sleeping most important now — IVC compression risk highest
  • Accept that perfect sleep is unlikely; prioritize rest over sleep

Mental Preparation

  • Birth plan development reduces anxiety
  • Childbirth education classes address fear of the unknown
  • Partner engagement is independently associated with better outcomes

Physical Monitoring

  • Establish baseline kick counts (10 movements in 2 hours)
  • Blood pressure surveillance increases — preeclampsia peaks late T3
  • Watch for preterm labor before week 37
Core Pillar 01

Nutrition

Food is the primary input signal for two ecosystems at once. The goal isn't eating perfectly — it's covering the nutrients that are genuinely difficult to get from diet alone and setting a rhythm that works with your symptoms.

Caloric Needs by Trimester

T1

First Trimester (Weeks 1–13)

No additional calories above pre-pregnancy baseline. If nausea is severe, just focus on tolerating food. Quality over quantity.

T2

Second Trimester (Weeks 14–27)

+340 calories/day above baseline. Focus on nutrient-dense sources — this is not 340 calories of crackers.

T3

Third Trimester (Weeks 28–40)

+450 calories/day. Protein targets become critical as fetal muscle development peaks. Small meals are essential — the stomach is physically compressed.

Foods to Prioritize

  • Colorful vegetables — especially leafy greens (spinach, kale, broccoli)
  • Whole grains over refined grains (fiber + nutrients)
  • Legumes: beans, lentils, chickpeas (iron, folate, fiber, protein)
  • Fatty fish 2–3×/week: salmon, sardines, trout, herring (low-mercury)
  • Eggs daily if tolerated (choline, protein, vitamin D, B12)
  • Full-fat dairy or fortified plant alternatives (calcium, iodine, D)
  • Nuts and seeds (magnesium, healthy fats, protein)
  • Berries and citrus (vitamin C enhances iron absorption)

Foods to Limit or Avoid

  • High-mercury fish: shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish, bigeye tuna
  • Raw or undercooked meat, poultry, seafood (listeria, salmonella, toxoplasma)
  • Unpasteurized dairy and soft cheeses (listeria)
  • Raw sprouts (E. coli, salmonella)
  • Processed meats unless heated to steaming (listeria)
  • Caffeine >200mg/day (~1 standard cup of coffee) per ACOG
  • Alcohol — no safe amount established
  • High-sodium processed foods (blood pressure, edema)
  • Added sugar and high-glycemic foods (GDM risk)

Meal Timing Strategy

  • 5–6 small meals rather than 3 large ones (manages nausea, heartburn, blood sugar)
  • Eat protein at every meal — stabilizes blood sugar and satiety
  • Breakfast within 30 minutes of waking — prevents nausea from empty stomach
  • Last meal 2–3 hours before bed — reduces heartburn
  • Pair iron-rich foods with vitamin C sources
  • Avoid calcium and coffee/tea within 1 hour of iron-rich meals

Hydration Target: 8–12 cups (64–96 oz) per day

ACOG recommendation. Total daily water intake target ~3L from all beverages and food. Supports amniotic fluid volume, prevents UTIs, reduces constipation, and counterintuitively helps reduce edema. Signs of adequate hydration: pale yellow urine. Increase in heat, humidity, or during exercise.

Critical Finding on Prenatal Vitamins A 2023 analysis of 421 prenatal supplements found that of 69 products containing six key nutrients, only one met ACOG target doses for all six. Most prenatal vitamins underdose iodine, choline, and omega-3. Supplement with choline and omega-3 (algae-based DHA if not eating fish) separately. Verify your prenatal contains 150 mcg iodine — this is the most commonly missing nutrient.
Core Pillar 02

Exercise

Movement is the most overlapping intervention in this entire guide. One practice that simultaneously reduces GDM risk, back pain, mood disorders, constipation, preeclampsia risk, and fatigue. If you only optimize one variable, make it this.

ACOG Recommendation

At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week for uncomplicated pregnancies. Regular exercise (3+ times/week) preferable to intermittent activity. Moderate intensity = able to hold a conversation, slightly breathless (RPE 12–14 on Borg scale).

General Safety Principles

  • Stay cool and hydrated — overheating, especially in the first trimester, is the primary risk
  • Avoid lying flat on your back after the first trimester (compresses IVC)
  • Avoid contact sports and high-fall-risk activities in all trimesters
  • Stop if you experience pain, bleeding, dizziness, shortness of breath, or chest pain

Evidence-Backed Protocols

Walking

The most accessible and evidence-supported intervention. 30 minutes most days reduces GDM risk, back pain, mood disorders, and constipation. Even 10-minute increments count toward the 150-minute weekly target.

Swimming / Water Aerobics

Non-weight-bearing protects joints; water pressure acts as natural compression for edema. Recommended as ideal third-trimester exercise. Comfort increases dramatically as belly grows.

Prenatal Yoga

Systematic review (BMC Pregnancy & Childbirth, 2022): benefits for anxiety, depression, low back pain, and sleep. Meta-analysis showed reduced in-labor cesarean rates. No adverse events in any published trial. Excellent for breath training applicable to labor.

Pelvic Floor Training (Kegels)

Protocol: 3 sets of 10–15 contractions (hold 3–10 sec each), 3×/day. 2024 meta-analysis confirmed reduced urinary incontinence and possible reduction in episiotomy/perineal tear rates. Start Week 1. Maintain throughout. Add perineal massage after 34 weeks.

Absolute Contraindications (per ACOG)

Severe anemia, unevaluated cardiac arrhythmia, placenta previa after 26 weeks, preterm labor, ruptured membranes, preeclampsia, incompetent cervix/cerclage, persistent second/third trimester bleeding. If any of these apply, exercise decisions must go through your provider.

Stop Exercise and Call Your Provider If: Vaginal bleeding, dizziness, shortness of breath before exertion begins, chest pain, headache, calf pain or swelling, preterm labor contractions, amniotic fluid leaking, or decreased fetal movement.

The evidence is clear on one thing: movement is medicine. 150 minutes a week touches more symptoms than any supplement.

Core Pillar 03

Sleep

Poor sleep in pregnancy is not merely uncomfortable — it's independently associated with gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, preterm birth, and postpartum depression. This is a physiological variable worth optimizing seriously.

Positioning: Left-Side Sleeping

Left-side sleeping is recommended from the second trimester onward. An IPD meta-analysis found an association between supine (back) sleeping and increased stillbirth risk, particularly in late pregnancy. Right-side sleeping is still considered safe — the goal is simply avoiding flat-back. A C-shaped or U-shaped pregnancy pillow or belly wedge + knee pillow achieves this with minimal friction.

Sleep Hygiene Adaptations

  • Consistent sleep and wake times — even through discomfort
  • Bedroom temperature 65–68°F (18–20°C) — metabolic demands make temperature regulation more critical
  • Eliminate screens 30–60 min before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin)
  • Wind-down routine: warm bath, light stretching, reading
  • Limit fluids 2 hours before bed to reduce nocturnal urination waking
  • Keep bathroom light dim at night — bright light causes significant arousal

CBT-I: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia — Gold Standard

The most evidence-supported non-pharmacological treatment for pregnancy insomnia. A 2024 RCT found that a 5-week CBT-I intervention (weeks 12–28) not only improved sleep but significantly reduced depressive symptoms at 6 months postpartum. CBT-I apps (e.g., Sleepio) can deliver key components digitally. Core components: sleep restriction, stimulus control, cognitive restructuring, relaxation training.

Supplements with Reasonable Safety Profiles

Magnesium glycinate 200–400mg before bed: Supports GABA activity, reduces muscle cramps, generally recognized as safe in pregnancy.

Melatonin: Limited data in pregnancy. Some providers cautiously approve short-term use of low doses (0.5–1mg). Melatonin does cross the placenta — consult your provider before using.

Rest Without Full Sleep For insomnia that cannot be fully resolved, 20–30 minute daytime naps reduce total sleep debt without significantly disrupting nighttime architecture. Accept that perfect sleep may be off the table in the third trimester — the goal shifts to maximizing rest.

Sleep isn't a luxury during pregnancy — it's infrastructure. Protect it like you'd protect any critical system.

Core Pillar 04

Stress Management

Chronic stress during pregnancy is not just a comfort issue — it's a physiological risk factor. Elevated cortisol is associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, and infant developmental outcomes. Managing stress is pregnancy healthcare.

Evidence-Based Interventions

Mindfulness-Based Interventions (MBSR/MBCT)

Multiple systematic reviews confirm benefits for anxiety, depression, and perceived stress. A comprehensive 2025 meta-analysis (Healthcare, MDPI) found significant reductions across all three measures. An 8-week MBCT program showed improvements in anxiety, depression, worry, and self-compassion. Even digital/app-based programs show benefit with less than 30 minutes per week of engagement (JMIR, 2025).

Exercise

Already documented across every other symptom category — moderate aerobic exercise is one of the most reliably effective interventions for mood and anxiety. 30 minutes most days. The mechanism is real and well-established.

Breathwork

Diaphragmatic breathing and 4-7-8 breath patterns activate the parasympathetic nervous system within minutes. No pregnancy-specific RCT data, but the physiological mechanism is well-established. Useful for acute anxiety and as labor preparation — a skill you'll actually use.

Social Support

One of the most robustly protective factors across all perinatal mental health research. Active maintenance of close relationships, participation in prenatal group classes, and peer support communities all matter. Partner involvement in prenatal care appointments is independently associated with better outcomes.

Nature Exposure

Emerging evidence suggests regular time outdoors in natural settings reduces cortisol and improves mood. Consistent with exercise benefits when walks are taken in parks or natural environments — an easy way to stack two interventions.

Journaling / Expressive Writing

Low-cost, zero-risk intervention with modest evidence for stress reduction. Particularly useful for processing anxieties about birth and parenting. No downside to trying it.

When to Seek Professional Help Anxiety or low mood persisting more than 2 weeks · Panic attacks or inability to function · Intrusive thoughts or fears that cannot be managed · History of depression, anxiety disorder, trauma, or eating disorder (all increase perinatal risk) · Any thoughts of self-harm. Perinatal mental health specialists, CBT therapists, and IPT practitioners have specific training in pregnancy-related presentations. Pharmacotherapy (SSRIs) is used when clinical need outweighs risk — this decision belongs to a qualified provider.

Every symptom is a signal. The goal isn't to silence them all — it's to understand what your ecosystem is telling you.

Symptom Guide

Symptom-by-Symptom Protocols

Here's what the evidence actually says for each common symptom — organized by intervention strength, not by what's easiest to recommend. Every body responds differently; treat these as experiments worth running.

Symptom 01

Nausea / Morning Sickness

Affects ~85% of pregnancies · Peaks week 9 · Typically resolves weeks 12–16

Nausea is one of the most common and most miserable early pregnancy experiences. The good news: several interventions have genuinely strong RCT evidence. You don't have to white-knuckle through it.

Symptom 05

Heartburn / Acid Reflux (GERD)

Affects ~66% of pregnant women · ~25% report daily symptoms · Progesterone relaxes the lower esophageal sphincter

GERD in pregnancy is mechanical and hormonal — the growing uterus physically pushes stomach acid upward while progesterone relaxes the valve that's supposed to stop it. Lifestyle modifications are genuinely first-line here.

Symptom 07

Insomnia / Sleep Disruption

Over 70% of pregnant women report insomnia symptoms · Physical discomfort, frequent urination, RLS, anxiety

Poor sleep during pregnancy is not merely discomfort — it's independently associated with gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, preterm birth, and postpartum depression. The CBT-I evidence is strong and worth investing in.

InterventionEvidenceNotes
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)Strong — Gold Standard2024 RCT: 5-week CBT-I also reduced postpartum depression at 6 months. Apps like Sleepio deliver digitally.
Left-side sleeping (second/third trimester)StrongImproves IVC blood flow; IPD meta-analysis associated supine sleep with increased stillbirth risk in late pregnancy
Pregnancy pillow / wedge supportConsensusSupports hips, knees, belly simultaneously; dramatically improves positional compliance
Sleep hygiene (dark room, 65–68°F, consistent schedule)ConsensusStandard CBT-I components apply in pregnancy; metabolic demands make temperature regulation more important
Limit fluids 2 hours before bedConsensusReduces nocturnal waking for urination — simple and effective

Note on Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) Commonly used for pregnancy insomnia but evidence for efficacy is weak. Reserve for severe cases and consult your provider. The CBT-I stack is more effective and has no risks to baby.
Symptom 08

Mood Changes / Anxiety / Depression

Antenatal depression: ~10–15% · Anxiety disorders: ~15–20% · Significantly underdiagnosed

Antenatal anxiety and depression are common, clinically significant, and treatable — but they're frequently dismissed as "normal pregnancy feelings." They have real consequences for fetal neurodevelopment if untreated. This is worth taking seriously.

InterventionEvidenceNotes
Regular aerobic exerciseStrongOne of the most evidence-supported interventions for mood; reduces depression and anxiety independently
Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)Moderate–StrongSystematic reviews show improvements in anxiety, depression, stress; 8-week programs most studied
Social support (maintained actively)Strong (epidemiological)One of the most consistently protective factors in all perinatal mental health research
Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA+DHA)ModerateEPA specifically has antidepressant properties; higher EPA:DHA ratios associated with better antidepressant effect
Sleep optimizationStrong (bidirectional)Sleep disruption worsens mood; mood disorders worsen sleep — treating either improves both
Psychotherapy (CBT, Interpersonal Therapy)StrongIPT is specifically validated for perinatal depression; CBT is broadly effective
Mindfulness mobile appsEmerging Moderate2025 JMIR RCT showed effectiveness for mild-moderate depression with <30 min/week engagement
When to Escalate Persistent low mood lasting over 2 weeks, inability to function, loss of interest in baby's wellbeing, or any thoughts of self-harm require immediate professional assessment. SSRIs are used in pregnancy when benefit outweighs risk — do not let stigma prevent treatment of severe antenatal depression. The risk of untreated depression is real; the risk of SSRIs is manageable and provider-guided.
Symptom 09

Gestational Diabetes Risk

Affects 6–9% of US pregnancies · Universal screening at 24–28 weeks (1-hour glucose challenge)

GDM is largely preventable through lifestyle — the evidence base here is unusually strong, with a 2024 network meta-analysis of 116 studies and 40,000+ participants. Diet + exercise is the combination that matters most.

InterventionEvidenceNotes
Combined diet + exercise interventionStrongNetwork meta-analysis of 116 studies, 40,940 participants (Nature Communications Medicine, 2024) — most effective combination
Low glycemic index (LGI) dietStrongSignificantly reduces 2-hour postprandial glucose; shown most effective for glucose control specifically
DASH dietStrongReduces fasting glucose; lowers macrosomia by 89% and cesarean rate by 46% in studies
Aerobic exercise (150 min/week)StrongGroup-based, facility-delivered programs show strongest effects vs. home-based
Resistance / strength trainingModerateImproves insulin sensitivity independently of aerobic exercise — both types matter
Dietary fiber (25–30g/day)ModerateSlows glucose absorption; reduces postprandial glucose excursions
Myoinositol supplementationModerateNetwork meta-analysis identified as effective; discuss with provider
Weight management within GWG guidelinesStrongExcess gestational weight gain is a primary modifiable GDM risk factor
Symptom 10

Preeclampsia Risk

Affects 5–8% of pregnancies · Leading cause of maternal and fetal morbidity · New-onset hypertension after 20 weeks

Preeclampsia is one of the more serious pregnancy complications — and it has a meaningful lifestyle prevention profile. The aspirin evidence in particular is very strong. This is a conversation to have with your provider early.

InterventionEvidenceNotes
Low-dose aspirin (81–162mg/day, before 16 weeks)Very Strong — USPSTF AReduces preeclampsia risk ~15% in high-risk women. Critical: must start early in pregnancy. Medical decision only.
Calcium supplementation (1–2g/day, low-intake populations)Strong49% reduction in preeclampsia risk vs. placebo; 30% reduction in gestational hypertension
Regular exerciseModerate–StrongNetwork meta-analysis (130 RCTs, 2022) identified exercise as effective preventive strategy
Vitamin D adequacyModerateStudies show benefit; optimal dose still under investigation
Healthy body weight / appropriate gestational weight gainModerateObesity is a primary risk factor; appropriate GWG matters
Low-sodium dietConsensusReduces hypertensive risk; paired with adequate hydration
Adequate hydrationModerateDehydration associated with higher preeclampsia risk in cohort studies
Medical Decision Aspirin for preeclampsia prevention is a risk-stratified medical decision made with your provider. Do not self-prescribe. The optimal window is 12–16 weeks — if you have risk factors (first pregnancy, obesity, prior preeclampsia, hypertension, diabetes, multifetal gestation), raise this with your OB before 16 weeks.
Symptom 11

Leg Cramps

Affects ~30–50% of pregnant women · Second and third trimesters · Often nocturnal

The honest picture: the evidence for leg cramp interventions is weaker than most other symptom categories. A 2020 Cochrane review found "very low quality" evidence across all oral interventions. Magnesium shows the most consistent directional benefit — worth trying at low risk.

InterventionEvidenceNotes
Magnesium supplementation (oral)Low–ModerateSome RCTs show reduction in cramp frequency; evidence inconsistent but magnesium glycinate 200–300mg before bed is a reasonable, low-risk trial
Calf stretches before bedConsensusStrong safety profile; consistently recommended despite limited formal RCT evidence — just do it
Magnesium-rich diet (leafy greens, nuts, seeds, whole grains)ConsensusSupports overall magnesium status; no downside
HydrationConsensusDehydration is a known cramp trigger; easy to address
Walking / movementConsensusImproves circulation to lower extremities
CalciumLow EvidenceNot shown to consistently reduce cramp frequency in trials
Vitamin D + Calcium comboLow EvidenceCochrane review found no effect on frequency, duration, or pain intensity
Evidence Transparency This is one area where the data doesn't support a confident recommendation. The 2020 Cochrane review (Luo et al.) found all oral interventions — magnesium, calcium, vitamin D, B vitamins, vitamin C — have "very low quality" evidence due to small sample sizes. We're telling you this because you deserve the honest picture, not just confident-sounding advice. Stretch your calves. Try magnesium if you want. Accept that this may be one you just manage with movement and warmth.
Emergency Reference

Red Flags — When to Seek Care Immediately

No lifestyle protocol replaces prenatal care. These symptoms require immediate medical evaluation — do not manage them at home.

Call 911 or Go to the Emergency Room

  • Sudden, severe headache — "worst of your life"
  • Changes in vision: blurring, spots, double vision, or loss of vision
  • Severe chest pain or difficulty breathing
  • Signs of stroke: facial drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty
  • Severe abdominal pain that does not resolve
  • Seizure
  • Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby
  • Loss of consciousness or fainting

Contact Your Provider Urgently — Same Day

  • Vaginal bleeding at any stage of pregnancy
  • Fluid leaking from the vagina (possible premature rupture of membranes)
  • Fever over 100.4°F / 38°C
  • Sudden, severe swelling of face or hands (possible preeclampsia)
  • Baby's movement has decreased or stopped — do not wait
  • Unilateral leg swelling with pain or redness (DVT screening)
  • Persistent, severe vomiting with inability to keep fluids down (hyperemesis gravidarum)
  • Burning or pain with urination (UTI can trigger preterm labor if untreated)
  • Regular uterine contractions before 37 weeks (preterm labor)
  • Severe heartburn unresponsive to lifestyle measures
  • Any feeling that "something is wrong" — trust your instinct
CDC HEAR HER Campaign Research shows pregnant and postpartum women are sometimes not taken seriously by providers. If you feel something isn't right, speak up. If your provider is dismissive of symptoms you're concerned about, seek a second opinion. Advocate for yourself — your instincts are a clinical data point.
